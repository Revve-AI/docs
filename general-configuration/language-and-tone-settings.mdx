Open your Chat Agent and select the **Basic** tab. **Languages** and **Communication Tone** sit in the left column, alongside **Role**.

![Languages and Communication Tone fields on the Basic settings tab](/screenshots/chatbot-basic-settings.png)

Both settings feed the model as system-prompt instructions, not as a separate translation or style layer — the model itself decides how to apply them to each reply.

## Languages

| Setting | Default | When to change |
|---------|---------|----------------|
| **Languages** | `English` only | Add a language when you expect visitors to write in it. At least one language must stay selected — the last badge can't be removed, and removing a badge and leaving zero triggers a toast rather than saving. |

The picker shows every language your team has enabled as removable badges, plus a **+ Add** dropdown for adding more. Today that list is **English** and **Vietnamese** — there's no free-text or unrestricted language input; you pick from what's configured for the team.

### How it works

The selected languages are written into the agent's system prompt as an instruction: reply in whichever supported language the visitor is using. There's no separate translation step — the underlying LLM does the detection and generation together, so quality depends on how well that model handles the language, not on a Revve-specific language engine.

If a visitor writes in a language you haven't selected, the agent falls back to one of your selected languages rather than attempting the unsupported one — so if you serve a mixed audience, keep English in the list as a safety net even when it isn't your primary language.

<Tip>
Your knowledge base content doesn't need to match every selected language. The agent will still try to answer in the visitor's language, translating from whatever your source material is written in — but translated answers are only as good as the model's fluency in that language pair. Preview a few real questions in each language you add before relying on it in production.
</Tip>

## Communication Tone

| Setting | Default | When to change |
|---------|---------|----------------|
| **Communication Tone** | Formal and Professional | Switch to **Casual and Friendly** for consumer-facing, lifestyle, or e-commerce brands where a business-formal register feels stiff. Keep **Formal and Professional** for regulated or B2B contexts (finance, legal, healthcare) where visitors expect a polished register. |

Communication Tone is a two-option dropdown — there's no custom or third tone today. It applies across every language the agent supports; there's no per-language tone override in the Basic tab UI.

For example, the same answer under each tone:

> **Formal and Professional:** "Thank you for your inquiry. Our Starter plan is priced at $29 per month and includes a comprehensive set of features to support your initial requirements."

> **Casual and Friendly:** "Hey! Great question — our Starter plan is $29/month and covers everything you need to get going."

<Tip>
Tone sets the register; **Role** (also on the Basic tab) sets the personality and domain framing. Use both together — Communication Tone alone won't make a generic bot sound like *your* brand.
</Tip>

## A configuration pass that works

1. **Match tone to your brand voice.** If your marketing copy is casual, a formal bot will feel like a mismatch to visitors coming from your site.
2. **Add languages you can actually verify.** Every language you enable is a language you should preview real conversations in — an untested language is a silent quality gap.
3. **Review live conversations.** Check conversation history periodically to confirm the language and tone combination is landing the way you expect.
